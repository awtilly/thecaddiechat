Mountain Ridge
Country Club

Donald Ross in rolling northern New Jersey — wide fairways, deceptive greens, and a century of history.

ArchitectDonald Ross
Year1929
LocationWest Caldwell, NJ
AccessPrivate
RestorationRon Prichard, 1998–2012

The Story

Mountain Ridge's founding story is rooted in exclusion. In 1912, two prominent Newark businessmen — A.J. Dimond, a steel magnate whose cranes helped build the Panama Canal, and Louis Bamberger, who built one of Newark's largest department stores — were refused playing privileges at area clubs because they were Jewish. They founded Mountain Ridge with 25 charter members from Newark's Progress Club. Since few members actually knew how to play golf, the official opening was celebrated with a softball game.

The original course was in West Orange on severely hilly terrain. A.W. Tillinghast expanded it to 18 holes in 1916–17, but the site proved too steep. Seth Raynor, Charles Banks, and Walter Travis were all consulted and delivered the same verdict: the land couldn't support a top course. The club sold the property and hired Donald Ross to design a new course on 250 acres in West Caldwell, opening in 1931.

"Now that Plainfield is receiving its just props, Mountain Ridge may be the most underrated course in golf-rich New Jersey." — Tom Doak

Architecture & Design

Classic Ross: wide fairways routed across gentle, rolling terrain with specimen maples and oaks. A meandering stream drifts in and out of range. The greens feature dramatic contours — subtle ledges, ridges, and tilts that make putting a cerebral exercise. Several holes nod to Old World templates: the 7th has a Punchbowl green, the 11th carries a touch of Biarritz.

Ron Prichard led a faithful restoration beginning in 1998, using Ross's original field sketches to locate lost bunkers, widen fairways to their original generous dimensions, and reinstate green contours that had eroded over decades. The course now measures just over 7,000 yards from the back tees. The magnificent fieldstone clubhouse — designed by Clifford Wendehack, the same architect behind the iconic clubhouses at Winged Foot and Bethpage — crowns the property.

What Stayed With Us

The 18th at Mountain Ridge is one of the best finishing holes in the state — 430-plus yards, climbing from the tee to a two-tiered green with the Wendehack clubhouse looming behind. It's the kind of hole where you know exactly what you have to do, and doing it is another matter entirely. The course rewards patience and precision in the way that only Ross courses can — it feels fair, it feels timeless, and it feels like it belongs exactly where it is.
