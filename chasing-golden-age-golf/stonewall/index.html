The Founding

Stonewall holds a special place in golf architecture history as Tom Doak's first solo design project. Opened in 1991 on the rolling farmland of Chester County, Pennsylvania, it represents the beginning of what would become one of the most influential careers in modern course architecture.

Doak, who had studied under Pete Dye and traveled the world documenting great courses for his book "The Confidential Guide to Golf Courses," finally had the chance to put his minimalist philosophy into practice.

Architectural Significance

What makes Stonewall remarkable is its restraint. In an era when many architects were building bold, heavily constructed courses, Doak went the opposite direction. The bunkers feel as though they were discovered, not built. The greens follow the natural contours of the hillsides.

The course plays through rolling meadows and wooded corridors, with sight lines that reveal themselves gradually. Every hole presents options — multiple routes to the green, risk-reward decisions that reward intelligence over raw power.

On the Ground

Walking Stonewall with my fellow Evans Scholars, the first thing that struck us was the quiet. There's no development around the course, no highway noise, no distractions. Just the land, the sky, and the golf.

The greens were the highlight — complex putting surfaces with subtle breaks that rewarded careful study and punished carelessness. This was where Doak's genius was most apparent.
