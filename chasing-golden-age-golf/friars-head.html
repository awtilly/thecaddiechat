08

Friar’s Head

Coore & Crenshaw’s modern masterpiece on Long Island’s North Shore — sand dunes, bluffs above the Sound, and no yardage markers.

ArchitectsBill Coore & Ben Crenshaw
Year2002
LocationBaiting Hollow, Long Island, NY
AccessPrivate
FounderKen Bakst (1997 USGA Mid-Am champion)

The Story

Friar’s Head was born from the vision of Ken Bakst, the 1997 U.S. Mid-Amateur champion, who identified 350 acres of extraordinary land on Long Island’s North Shore. The property featured two starkly contrasting environments: dramatic sand dunes with 150–200 foot bluffs overlooking Long Island Sound, and flat former potato farmland to the south.

Coore and Crenshaw were introduced to the property in 1997, fresh off their game-changing Sand Hills project. They refused to rush — taking over a year before committing, making numerous site visits until they solved the routing puzzle. They were philosophically opposed to creating another “Jekyll and Hyde” course. The solution was a figure-eight routing that moves fluidly between sand and meadow. Clearing alone took five months before a single shovel of dirt was moved.

Bill Coore said Friar’s Head proved to be the best property he and Crenshaw had ever worked with, other than Sand Hills.

Architecture & Design

This is a par 71 with no yardage markers on the scorecard — a deliberate choice reflecting Coore and Crenshaw’s belief in golf’s purest form. Greens range from 3,000 to 18,000 square feet. Bunkers feel like natural formations, and the fescue, sand, and dense bushes create strategic complexity without artifice.

The back nine is where the drama builds. The par-five 14th rises through forested dunes toward a steeply pitched green. The 15th drops from an elevated tee through a natural valley toward Long Island Sound — and from the 15th green, an elevated boardwalk takes you around the dunes high above the shoreline to the 16th tee. It is one of the most beautiful walks in golf. The finishing stretch includes a sinister short par-three 17th and a strong par-four 18th that bends around the shoulder of a giant hill.

What Stayed With Us

Friar’s Head isn’t technically a Golden Age course — it opened in 2002. But it’s built in the spirit and philosophy of the era: let the land speak, move as little earth as possible, and trust the golfer to engage on the course’s terms. Walking from the 15th green across that boardwalk, high above Long Island Sound with Connecticut visible in the distance, you understand why people put this course alongside Cypress Point and Pine Valley. It’s not hyperbole. It’s just that good. And it was the perfect way to end the chase.
