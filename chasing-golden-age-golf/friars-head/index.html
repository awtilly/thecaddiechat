The Founding

Friar's Head, perched on the North Shore of Long Island, is the work of Bill Coore and Ben Crenshaw — the modern architects most often credited with reviving the minimalist principles of the Golden Age. Opened in 2002, it has quickly risen to become one of the most highly regarded courses in America.

The property is extraordinary — dramatic bluffs overlooking the Long Island Sound, rolling terrain that transitions from links-style open land to wooded upland, and a sense of isolation despite being only 70 miles east of Manhattan.

Architectural Significance

Coore and Crenshaw are disciples of the Golden Age architects, and Friar's Head is perhaps their most complete expression of that philosophy. The course moves through multiple distinct landscapes — open, windswept holes along the bluffs give way to inland holes framed by mature trees.

The green complexes are world-class. Wide, undulating, and full of subtle contours, they reward creativity and punish one-dimensional thinking. Many greens can be approached with a variety of shots, giving players freedom to play their game.

On the Ground

Friar's Head was the culmination of our Northeast trip. Standing on the bluffs, looking out over the Sound with the wind in your face, you understand why people become obsessed with this game. The variety was stunning — no two holes felt alike, yet every hole felt like it belonged. If Stonewall was the beginning of our journey into minimalist architecture, Friar's Head was the culmination.
