North Jersey
Country Club

Walter Travis on severe, rolling terrain — bold greens and blind shots from one of golf’s first great champions.

ArchitectWalter Travis
Year1921
LocationWayne, NJ
AccessPrivate

The Story

North Jersey CC is the “father” of golf in its section of New Jersey. Play began in March 1895 as the Paterson Golf Club, making it one of the state’s oldest clubs. Its first president, Garret A. Hobart, later served as Vice President of the United States. A fire destroyed the original clubhouse in 1917, and the club rebuilt and commissioned Walter Travis to design the current course in 1921.

Travis was no ordinary architect. He was a three-time U.S. Amateur champion (1900, 1901, 1903) and the 1904 British Amateur champion — the first American citizen to win that title. Known as “The Old Man” because he didn’t pick up a golf club until age 34, he brought a player’s intelligence to every design.

Architecture & Design

Travis built the course on severely hilly terrain, embracing blind shots and wild green contours that fit the rolling property perfectly. Tom Doak noted in The Confidential Guide that the course features bold Travis greens and strategic shot values — though he lamented that younger members have been slowly chipping away at the course’s most distinctive features over time.

Robert Trent Jones remodeled portions in 1978, and Brian Silva renovated bunkers in 1994. About a dozen of Travis’s original greens remain — inventive, unpredictable putting surfaces that reward imagination over power. The Wendehack clubhouse connects it architecturally to Winged Foot and Mountain Ridge.

What Stayed With Us

North Jersey is a course that forces you to think. The blind shots feel daring rather than unfair, and when you finally read a Travis green correctly, you feel like you’ve cracked a code. The hilly terrain makes the walk feel earned, and the views from the high points remind you that even 20 miles from Manhattan, there’s still wild, dramatic golf to be found.
