06

Hollywood
Golf Club

Walter Travis’s masterpiece near the Jersey Shore — 175 bunkers, brilliant green complexes, and one of golf’s great restorations.

ArchitectWalter Travis (1917 redesign)
Year1898 / redesigned 1917
LocationDeal, NJ
AccessPrivate
RestorationBrian Schneider / Renaissance Golf, 2013–2021

The Story

Hollywood Golf Club was established in 1898 by New York City businessmen who spent summers along the North Jersey Shore. The club went through three locations before settling on its current 300-acre property in 1913, where Isaac Mackie laid out the initial course. Dissatisfied, the club hired Walter Travis in 1916 to completely redesign it. Travis kept most of Mackie’s routing corridors but overhauled the bunkers, green complexes, and strategic angles so thoroughly that the course became entirely his.

Over decades, renovations by Dick Wilson (1956), Geoffrey Cornish (1980s), and Rees Jones (1998) gradually obscured Travis’s original intent. Trees grew thicker, coastal breezes were blocked, and the linksland identity was lost. Beginning in 2013, Brian Schneider of Tom Doak’s Renaissance Golf Design led a meticulous restoration using 1940 aerial overlays and Frank Barrett’s original green committee notes. When the club asked if they should add Doak’s name to the scorecard, Schneider told them to take everyone’s name off except Travis’s.

A.W. Tillinghast once joked that on a Travis course, if you hit a bad shot, you didn’t need to ask the caddie where the ball was — “I know it’s in a bunker.”

Architecture & Design

Hollywood is now considered one of the finest examples of Walter Travis’s genius. His green complexes are among the most intricate of any architect in history — walking the course feels like moving through a museum of putting surface artistry. Travis’s philosophy was revolutionary: hazards should be out of play for the 100-shooter but dangerously in play for the scratch golfer.

The property sits within two miles of the Atlantic Ocean on sandy ground. With trees thinned to Travis’s original intent, coastal breezes now play a proper role again. The famous 12th hole — the “Heinz 57 Hole” — originally had 57 bunkers. Schneider’s team restored 39 of them. The visual from the tee is both stunning and bewildering.

What Stayed With Us

Hollywood is the course that made us rethink Walter Travis. Before standing on the 12th tee and seeing 39 bunkers framing a single hole, we knew Travis intellectually. After Hollywood, we felt him. Every green is a puzzle, every bunker is a conversation. And knowing that this restoration brought his original vision back from near-extinction makes the experience feel almost sacred. This is what the best of golf preservation looks like.
