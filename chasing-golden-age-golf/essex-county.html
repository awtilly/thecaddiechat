Essex County
Country Club

Three Golden Age masters — Tillinghast, Raynor, Banks — harmonized on New Jersey’s oldest country club grounds.

ArchitectsTillinghast / Raynor / Banks
Year1917 / 1926
LocationWest Orange, NJ
AccessPrivate
RenovationGil Hanse, 2024

The Story

The oldest country club in New Jersey and the sixth oldest in the United States, founded in 1887. Originally a hunt club centered around the Orange Mineral Springs, Essex County introduced golf in 1898. In 1900, the club hosted the meeting that birthed the New Jersey State Golf Association.

Tillinghast designed a new 18-hole course on the current mountaintop site in 1917 — his success there is widely credited with springboarding his career to commissions at Winged Foot, Baltusrol, and San Francisco Golf Club. In the mid-1920s, Seth Raynor was brought in to expand to 36 holes. He kept seven Tillinghast originals (Nos. 1–6 and 9) and designed eleven new ones. When Raynor died in 1926, Charles Banks completed the build — one of his first solo projects. Gil Hanse returned in 2023–24 for a major renovation.

The genius of Essex County is how three distinct architectural voices — Tillinghast’s sophistication, Raynor’s boldness, Banks’s drama — harmonize into a single, cohesive experience.

Architecture & Design

The front nine features sophisticated, low-lying Tillinghast holes that use natural terrain and flanking bunkers. The transition happens seamlessly at holes 7–8, with Raynor/Banks taking over. The back nine is the showstopper: bold template holes including a Redan, Double Plateau, Alps, Punchbowl, and Eden. The 5th hole features cross bunkers that force creative ground-game approaches — watching the ball land 20–50 yards short and trundle toward the flag is one of the most thrilling shots in the region.

The course is built atop a massive hill with minimal earth-moving (bunkers, mounds, and green pads only), preserving the raw, vintage aesthetic. Francis A. Byrne Golf Course, the public course next door, was originally ECCC’s “lower course.”

What Stayed With Us

Standing on the back nine at Essex County, you can feel the shift from Tillinghast to Raynor. The front eases you in with elegance — sophisticated, intrinsic to the land. Then the back hits you with drama. It should feel disjointed. Instead, it feels like a conversation between masters, each building on what came before. That’s what makes this place special — three of the greatest architects in history left their fingerprints here, and somehow the course is greater than any of them alone.
