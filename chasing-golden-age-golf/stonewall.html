Stonewall

Tom Doak's first private design — strategic restraint on a former dairy farm outside Philadelphia.

Architect Tom Doak (with Gil Hanse)
Year 1993
Location Elverson, PA
Access Private

The Story

Stonewall is the course that launched Tom Doak's career — his first private design, built on a former dairy farm in Chester County, Pennsylvania. The club's cow-and-golf-club logo is a nod to that agricultural heritage. Tom Fazio was originally hired for the project but pulled out due to commitments at Shadow Creek, and the untried Doak stepped in. He was so committed that he moved his young family to the area to personally supervise construction.

The property is a quiet, unpretentious stretch of rolling countryside dotted with old stone walls and fieldstone outbuildings — the kind of place where you can play 'til dusk, clean up for dinner, and stay the night in one of the club's cozy cottages. That pastoral vision came from founder Jack May, and Doak honored it completely.

"The members they'd signed up at the beginning were all members of all those places — Pine Valley, Merion, Aronimink. And they definitely wanted it to fit in the neighborhood."

Architecture & Design

The Old Course plays 6,359 yards with a 138 slope from the Blues — walking only, always. Doak largely kept Fazio's original routing but made key changes, most notably reversing the direction of the 18th hole, which sweeps down a broad hillside and finishes in the shadow of a weathered stone building. When the club wanted to demolish that building, Doak made everyone march up the hill to see how it framed the view — and saved it.

The greens are smallish and set at grade, giving the course a natural flow. Subtle homages to Philadelphia's classic courses are woven throughout — a contour on the 2nd green was inspired by the 7th at Merion. The 4th hole is a standout downhill-then-uphill par four with a shallow green reminiscent of Augusta National's 12th. The par-three 5th offers panoramic hilltop views.

The North Course (aka "The Udder Course") arrived ten years later with a different brief: wider fairways but more complicated, deceptive green complexes. The 2016 U.S. Mid-Amateur used both courses for the 36-hole final — a first for the championship.

What Stayed With Us

Stonewall isn't a course that beats you up — it's one that draws you in. The challenge is layered and strategic: most tee shots are manageable, but knowing which part of the fairway sets up the approach is another level of complexity entirely. Beyond the architecture, it's the atmosphere that lingers. The rich countryside, the easy camaraderie by the second tee, the feeling of golf stripped back to its essentials. This is what the game should feel like.
