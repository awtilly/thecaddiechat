07

Winged Foot
Golf Club

Tillinghast’s crown jewel — six U.S. Opens, the most celebrated greens in golf, and a reputation as intimidating as they come.

ArchitectA.W. Tillinghast
Year1923
LocationMamaroneck, NY
AccessPrivate
RestorationGil Hanse, 2016–17

The Story

Winged Foot was founded in 1921 by members of the New York Athletic Club who wanted their own golf haven. The name and winged foot logo come from a sculpture in the NYAC lobby. They hired A.W. Tillinghast to design two “man-sized courses,” and he delivered exactly that — the East and West opened in 1923.

The West Course was recognized almost immediately. Just six years after opening, it hosted the 1929 U.S. Open — no course so young has ever been selected before or since. Bobby Jones won that championship in dramatic fashion, defeating Al Espinosa by 23 strokes in a 36-hole playoff. In the decades since, the West has hosted Phil Mickelson’s infamous 2006 collapse and Bryson DeChambeau’s dominant 2020 victory — each adding another chapter to the legend.

Architecture & Design

Tillinghast built Winged Foot on relatively flat terrain with rocky soil, but his genius shines through in one of the most celebrated sets of greens in the world. The putting surfaces are large and subtly contoured with troughs, spines, and multiple distinct pin positions. The 2016–17 Gil Hanse restoration expanded the greens by 26% using historical aerials and original 1923 photographs, recovering lost features and strategic complexity.

The first green alone has a trough snaking through its center that creates four distinct sections. Every hole demands precision with approach shots — this is a second-shot golf course in the truest sense. Tillinghast’s four courses within a 4-mile stretch of Old White Plains Road (Winged Foot East and West, Quaker Ridge, Fenway) represent one of the greatest concentrations of a single architect’s work anywhere in the world.

Geoff Shackelford called Tillinghast “the most eccentric and fascinating character of the Golden Age” of golf course architecture.

What Stayed With Us

You feel the weight of Winged Foot before you hit a shot. It’s in the stone of the Wendehack clubhouse, in the names carved on the championship plaques, in the way the 18th green sits there waiting for you at the end of 430 yards of climbing fairway. The greens are terrifying and beautiful in equal measure. We walked off understanding exactly why this place has hosted six U.S. Opens — and why it will host a seventh. Some courses earn their reputation over time. Winged Foot earned it immediately and has never let go.
