The Founding

The Francis A. Byrne Golf Course carries one of the most remarkable origin stories in American municipal golf. Originally designed by Seth Raynor, one of the greatest architects of the Golden Age, the course was completed by his associate Charles Banks after Raynor's sudden death in 1926.

What makes this extraordinary is that a course of this architectural pedigree is open to the public. Raynor and Banks were known for designing courses at the most exclusive private clubs — yet here, their template holes and strategic genius are accessible to anyone.

Architectural Significance

The Byrne course features several of Raynor's famous template holes — designs borrowed and refined from the great links courses of Scotland and England. These templates, including variations of the Redan, Biarritz, and Cape holes, represent some of the most influential ideas in golf course design.

Banks, who completed the construction after Raynor's death, was a faithful steward of his mentor's vision while adding his own touches to the green complexes.

On the Ground

Playing Byrne was a revelation. Here was a public course with the bones of a private club masterpiece. The template holes were recognizable and challenging, the routing was clever, and the green complexes demanded precision.
